\section{Introduction}

A news caption and its article describe the same event, but from very
different vantage points: an article runs to hundreds of words; a caption
to roughly twenty.
Understanding how much article content a caption captures matters for
automatic caption generation, misinformation detection, and multimodal
summarisation.

We ask five questions: (RQ1) Do structural caption features predict
similarity? (RQ2) Does caption function affect alignment? (RQ3) Do named
entities provide a reliable alignment signal? (RQ4) Does similarity vary
by news category? (RQ5) Do UK and US outlets differ in caption--article
alignment?

Corresponding hypotheses: (H1) Structural features correlate significantly
with at least one similarity metric. (H2) Caption type significantly
predicts similarity. (H3) PERSON entity overlap is highest among entity
types. (H4) Similarity differs across news categories. (H5) UK outlets
(Guardian, BBC) show higher alignment than US outlets (WaPo, USA Today).

\section{Data and Method}

\subsection{Dataset}
We draw 30,000 caption--article pairs from VisualNews \citep{liu2020visualnews}
(stratified sample: 5,000 per category), covering Guardian and BBC News across
six topic categories. Full descriptive statistics are reported in
Table~\ref{tab:desc} in the Results section.

\subsection{Similarity Metrics}
All text is preprocessed with spaCy \texttt{en\_core\_web\_trf}:
lowercased, lemmatised, stopwords and punctuation removed.
Articles are truncated to 512 tokens for TF-IDF and Jaccard, and to 128
tokens for SBERT (within the model's optimal range).

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{2pt}
  \item \textbf{TF-IDF cosine} ($s_{tf}$): sklearn \texttt{TfidfVectorizer}
    (unigrams + bigrams, $\text{min\_df}=2$, $\text{max\_df}=0.95$) fit on the
    combined caption + article corpus; per-pair cosine similarity.
  \item \textbf{Jaccard} ($s_{jac}$): $|C \cap A|\;/\;|C \cup A|$ on lemma
    sets; 0 if union is empty.
  \item \textbf{SBERT} ($s_{sbert}$): cosine similarity of
    \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} embeddings \citep{reimers2019sbert}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Extraction}
Structural features (length, complexity, vocabulary) are extracted from
captions only using spaCy. POS-overlap features compare caption lemmas against
article lemmas per tag (NOUN, VERB, ADJ, PROPN). Named entities (PERSON, GPE,
ORG, DATE) are aligned with Levenshtein fuzzy matching (threshold 0.85,
\texttt{rapidfuzz}).

\subsection{Statistical Tests}
Pearson $r$ for correlations (RQ1); Kruskal--Wallis $H$ with
$\eta^2 = (H-k+1)/(n-k)$ for group comparisons (RQ2, RQ4, RQ5);
Dunn post-hoc with Bonferroni correction for pairwise source comparisons.

\subsection{RQ1: Structural Features and Similarity}

Figure~\ref{fig:struct_heat} shows Pearson $r$ for each structural feature
against the three similarity metrics; non-significant cells ($p > 0.05$) are
marked \textit{ns}. Figure~\ref{fig:reg_coefs} shows standardised OLS
coefficients ($\beta$) for SBERT as the outcome.

All features show at least one significant correlation, confirming H1.
The effects differ substantially by metric.

\textbf{POS overlap features} are the strongest and most consistent
predictors. Proper-noun overlap has the largest standardised coefficient
($\hat{\beta} = 0.28$, $p < 0.001$), followed by noun overlap
($\hat{\beta} = 0.19$). These features correlate significantly with all
three metrics ($r_{sbert} = 0.33$ and $0.29$ respectively), indicating
that captions sharing named referents with their article are more aligned
across all dimensions of similarity.

\textbf{Syntactic complexity} also predicts SBERT: average sentence length
($\hat{\beta} = 0.09$) and dependency depth ($\hat{\beta} = 0.08$) both
reach significance, suggesting that structurally richer captions tend to
mirror the article more closely at the semantic level.

\textbf{Caption length features} (word count, token count, character count)
correlate strongly with Jaccard ($r \approx 0.57$) but weakly with SBERT
($r \approx 0.08$), a well-known length bias in set-overlap metrics. Jaccard
values should therefore be interpreted with caption length in mind.

\textbf{Type--token ratio} (TTR) is negatively associated with all three
metrics ($r_{jac} = -0.34$, $\hat{\beta} = -0.02$), consistent with
lexically diverse captions introducing framing not present in the article.

\subsection{RQ4: News Category}

Figures~\ref{fig:cat_bar} and \ref{fig:cat_box}, and Table~\ref{tab:kw},
show how similarity varies across the six topic categories.

Differences are statistically significant for all three metrics
($p < 0.001$ in every case), though effect sizes are modest
($\eta^2 = 0.004$ for TF-IDF; $\eta^2 = 0.022$ for Jaccard;
$\eta^2 = 0.028$ for SBERT). The pattern differs across metrics.
\textbf{Sport} (median SBERT~=~0.52) and \textbf{business and economy}
(0.52) show the strongest semantic alignment, while
\textbf{entertainment and culture} (0.42) shows the weakest. Dunn
post-hoc tests (Bonferroni corrected) confirm that entertainment
differs significantly from every other category ($p < 0.001$), and that
sport and business differ from politics ($p < 0.01$).

These results align with the hard/soft news framework
\citep{LehmanWilzig2010}: event-driven, factually dense categories
(sport, business, politics) show stronger caption--article coherence,
while entertainment---closer to soft news in urgency and audience
orientation---shows the weakest alignment. The modest $\eta^2$ values
suggest journalistic format conventions constrain similarity regardless
of topic, limiting the practical magnitude of category-driven differences.
H4 is supported.

\subsection{RQ5: Cross-Source Variation}

The present corpus contains only two sources (BBC and Guardian), both
British, so H5's UK-vs-US comparison cannot be directly tested.
Figure~\ref{fig:src_bar} and Table~\ref{tab:kw} compare them.

Guardian captions score higher than BBC across all three metrics:
TF-IDF (0.217 vs.\ 0.179; $H = 538.2$, $p < 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.018$),
Jaccard (0.051 vs.\ 0.039; $H = 420.8$, $p < 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.014$),
and SBERT (0.476 vs.\ 0.458; $H = 66.0$, $p < 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.002$).
The lexical gap (TF-IDF, Jaccard) is partly a caption-length artefact:
Guardian captions average 18.5 words vs.\ BBC's 13.0, and length is a
strong Jaccard predictor ($r = 0.57$, RQ1). The near-zero SBERT $\eta^2$
indicates semantic alignment is essentially equivalent across the two
outlets once length differences are set aside. Figure~\ref{fig:src_heat}
further shows that this pattern holds across categories.
H5 cannot be fully assessed with only UK sources, but there is no evidence
of meaningful semantic alignment differences between outlets.
