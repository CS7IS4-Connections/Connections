\begin{abstract}

News image captions serve as a critical bridge between visual and textual
content, yet the textual relationship between captions and their parent
articles remains underexplored. We present a multi-dimensional empirical
analysis of caption--article textual similarity across 30,000 pairs drawn
from the VisualNews dataset \citep{liu2021visualnews}, covering the Guardian
and BBC News across six topic categories. Three complementary similarity
metrics are employed: TF-IDF cosine similarity, lemma-set Jaccard
similarity, and SBERT semantic similarity \citep{Reimers2019}. We extract
structural caption features, develop and apply a novel functional caption
typology (Extractive, Descriptive, Expansive, Independent) grounded in
\citet{marsh2003taxonomy}, perform named entity alignment analysis, and
conduct comparative analyses across news categories and publication sources.
Caption function emerges as the strongest predictor of similarity: extractive
captions (37.3\% of the corpus) achieve a mean SBERT similarity of 0.574,
nearly 0.17 points above independent captions ($\eta^2 = 0.194$). Among
structural predictors, proper-noun overlap dominates ($\hat{\beta} = 0.170$,
together with entity Jaccard $\hat{\beta} = 0.133$), reflecting the central
role of shared named referents. Category effects are significant but modest
($\eta^2 = 0.028$): sport and business show the highest caption--article
coherence; entertainment shows the weakest, consistent with the hard/soft
news distinction. Source differences in surface metrics are largely a
caption-length artefact, with negligible semantic differences between outlets.
All five hypotheses are supported.

\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Text Similarity, News Image Captioning,
Caption--Article Similarity, Named Entity Recognition, VisualNews,
Caption Typology, Multimodal News Analysis

\section{Introduction}

In modern news media, image captions do far more than describe photographs.
They summarise events, add context, and sometimes introduce information
absent from the article itself. Most existing research has focused on
automatic caption \textit{generation} --- training models to produce captions
from images and article text --- rather than analysing the textual
relationship between human-written captions and their source articles. This
leaves a significant gap: we know relatively little about how captions relate
linguistically to the articles they accompany, or how this relationship varies
across news topics and publishers.

This gap matters on two fronts. From a journalism perspective, understanding
caption--article similarity can reveal editorial conventions and inform best
practices in news writing. From a computational standpoint, characterising
this relationship supports downstream tasks such as misinformation detection,
multimodal retrieval, and automated captioning quality assessment. This study
addresses the gap systematically by measuring caption--article textual
similarity across multiple dimensions: lexical, semantic, structural, and
entity-based.

We used the VisualNews dataset \citep{liu2021visualnews}, which contains over
1.2 million news items from four major English-language news organisations
(The Guardian, BBC, Washington Post, and USA Today). Our analysis is conducted
on a 30,000-pair stratified sample drawn from the Guardian and BBC, covering
six topic categories. We quantify caption--article similarity using three
complementary measures, extract structural features that predict similarity,
develop a functional typology classifying captions by their rhetorical
relationship to the article, analyse named entity alignment as an indicator
of semantic coherence, and investigate how these patterns differ across news
categories and sources.

\subsection{Research Questions}

\noindent\textbf{Main Research Question:} What are the key factors that
explain the degree of textual (dis)similarity between news image captions
and their corresponding article texts, across news sources and domains?

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{RQ1 (Structural Features)}\\
How do structural properties of captions relate to their degree of
textual similarity with the corresponding article?

\item \textbf{RQ2 (Functional Classification)}\\
Can captions be classified into functional types, and do these types show
different similarity profiles?

\item \textbf{RQ3 (Named Entity Alignment)}\\
Which named entity types are most and least preserved between caption and
article, and does entity overlap predict overall semantic similarity?

\item \textbf{RQ4 (News Category Effects)}\\
Does caption--article similarity vary across news domains?

\item \textbf{RQ5 (Cross-Source Variation)}\\
Do similarity patterns hold uniformly across news sources?

\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

COSMOROE is a corpus-based framework for classifying semantic interrelations
between images, language and body movements, validated on a corpus of TV
travel programmes. \cite{pastra2008} is one of the earliest theoretically
grounded attempts to systematically categorize cross-modal relationships,
making it a useful foundation for caption typology for functional
classification into distinct types like descriptive, expansive, independent or
extractive. However, its domain is audiovisual broadcast media and it offers
no textual similarity metrics or caption-specific functional categories. It
provides theoretical evidence but requires substantial adaptation for a
text-to-text news caption context.

\cite{alikhani2020cross} annotates 10{,}000 image--caption pairs with
discourse-inspired coherence relations and uses them to train
coherence-aware captioning models, demonstrating improved caption consistency
when functional caption type is considered. It offers a grounded typology of
caption content relationships that closely relates to the
descriptive/expansive/extractive/independent classification developed in this
study. The limitation is that it operates on image--caption pairs in
general-domain datasets, not on text-to-text caption--article relationships
in news. Its coherence categories may not translate cleanly to journalistic
caption functions without deliberate adaptation.

\cite{biten2019goodnews} proposes a news image captioning model that
selectively draws on article text guided by visual cues and dynamically
incorporates out-of-vocabulary named entities, introducing the GoodNews
dataset. It is highly relevant to RQ2 by showing that news captions are
contextually grounded in article content in ways generic captions are not.
By demonstrating that persons, locations and organisations from articles are
regularly incorporated into captions it also aligns with RQ3. The core
limitation is that captioning is treated as a generation task, so
human-written caption-to-article textual similarity is never measured and
variation by news category or source is not examined.

\cite{wang2020measurement} reviews text similarity methods across two
dimensions --- text distance and text representation --- tracing the evolution
from edit distance and Jaccard similarity to BERT and graph neural networks.
This serves as the methodological backbone for informing metric selection
across all sub-questions. Its key limitation is being entirely
domain-agnostic, with no treatment of news-specific or short-text similarity
challenges. It also predates more recent sentence-level models like
Sentence-BERT, which are better suited for caption-length text comparisons.
\cite{luo2021newsclippings} introduces a large-scale dataset of authentic
but deliberately mismatched image--caption pairs designed to study
out-of-context misinformation in news media. It implicitly surfaces
distinctions between extractive and self-contained captions and the dataset
spans multiple news categories. However, the paper's focus is visual-textual
mismatch detection, not textual similarity measurement between captions and
articles.

\cite{marsh2003taxonomy} constructs a taxonomy of 49 image-to-text
relationship types derived from journalism, education, children's literature
and library science, validated on nearly 1{,}000 image--text pairs from news
and web pages. It is foundational to RQ2 as one of the most comprehensive
classification frameworks for how captions functionally relate to accompanying
text, with journalism explicitly among its source domains. However, it
addresses image--text rather than text-to-text relations and provides no
quantitative similarity metrics, making it best used as a theoretical anchor
for our operationalisations. \cite{yang2021journalistic} proposes a news
captioning model that explicitly encodes journalistic writing conventions,
achieving state-of-the-art performance on caption generation and named entity
metrics. It informs RQ1 by showing that caption structure follows professional
conventions and that journalistic captions are systematically context-dependent
through its entity-focused evaluation. Its primary limitation is the
generation framing, as it does not measure textual similarity between
human-written captions and articles, nor does it analyse variation by news
category.

\cite{vempati2019understanding} proposes a framework of eight semantic
image--text relation classes characterised using cross-modal mutual
information, semantic correlation and status relation metrics, with a deep
learning model to predict the classes automatically. It advances the field by
operationalising functional caption-text typologies into computationally
controlled categories. The limitation is that the metrics rely on visual
features that cannot be directly transferred to a text-to-text
caption--article framework, and the classification is not evaluated within
the news domain. \cite{mullerbanek2021multimodal} quantifies coherence between
news images and article text by applying named entity linking to extract
persons, locations and events and then computing cross-modal consistency
scores, validated across datasets covering multiple languages, topics and
domains, and demonstrating that consistency varies across topic domains
including politics, sports, economy and travel. The key limitation is that the
paper targets visual-textual fake news detection, not linguistic analysis of
caption--article textual similarity, and the entity measures are grounded in
computer vision rather than NLP-based textual overlap.

Recent work in multimodal AI has moved from treating images and text as
loosely associated modalities to modelling them as tightly coupled discourse
units, particularly in news settings where captions, images and articles
jointly construct meaning. Within this trajectory, our study focuses on the
specific textual relationship between news captions and their parent articles,
drawing on classic work on term specificity, contemporary entity-aware news
captioning, and coherence-based theories of image--text relations.

\cite{LehmanWilzig2010} proposed a third category of news, \textit{integrated
news}, defined as important but not urgent content falling between high-impact
hard news (politics, economy) and light soft news (gossip, human interest).
This framework is useful for investigating category effects by analysing news
in terms of urgency, factual density and audience targeting, suggesting that
news value influences the similarity patterns between captions and article
content across different news types.

\citet{Cheema2023} point out that meaning in news is shaped by news values
rather than simple redundancy between image and text. Combining semiotic
analysis with journalism theory, they emphasise that news values such as
prominence, negativity and impact influence how information is foregrounded.
This mechanism may vary between hard and soft news: hard news, driven by
timeliness and factual impact, may favour close alignment between textual
elements, whereas soft news, oriented toward human interest and emotional
expression, may present more elaborative or interpretive relations. This
provides theoretical grounding for our RQ4 and RQ5 analyses.

\citet{SparckJones1972TermSpecificity}'s seminal paper on term specificity
provides the foundational rationale for using weighted lexical similarity as
a baseline metric. She demonstrates that weighting terms inversely by
collection frequency yields marked improvements in retrieval effectiveness,
underlying the TF-IDF representations we adopt. \citet{Gomaa2013} categorise
text similarity algorithms into string-based, corpus-based and knowledge-based
types, emphasising the value of hybrid approaches. \citet{Reimers2019} present
Sentence-BERT, which employs a Siamese network architecture to generate
efficient and semantically robust sentence embeddings, significantly improving
performance on semantic textual similarity. Together, these studies establish
a developmental trajectory from superficial lexical matching to contextual
semantic modelling, directly informing our combination of TF-IDF, Jaccard
and SBERT.

\citet{liu2021visualnews} introduce VisualNews and demonstrate that news
images differ from generic captioning benchmarks because communicatively
salient named entities dominate the caption vocabulary. This empirically
motivates RQ3 by framing entities as central to news caption informativeness,
justifying entity overlap and entity Jaccard as proxies for caption--article
coherence. \citet{Qu2024VACNIC} sharpen this focus by showing that effective
news captions typically encode more context from the article than from the
image alone, underscoring the importance of article context for high-quality
news captions. \citet{Tranetal2020} further demonstrate that the accuracy of
PERSON entity prediction is a central factor in caption coherence, while
entity types such as ORG are more difficult to preserve accurately. This
directly informs our hypothesis that PERSON entities show the highest
caption--article overlap.

Finally, \citet{Alikhani2023ImageTextCoherence} on image--text coherence
provide the main theoretical scaffold for how we conceptualise caption
function and cross-category variation. Drawing on discourse coherence theory,
they argue that each image--text pair instantiates a specific pragmatic
relation, and that making these relations explicit supports better prediction,
generation, and evaluation of multimodal content. Their framework is
particularly important for RQ2 and RQ4, motivating the treatment of caption
functions as systematic and classifiable, and suggesting that genre and topic
shift the distribution of coherence relations. Our study adapts their
coherence-based perspective to a text-analytic setting, operationalising
functional types on captions and relating them to similarity scores, entity
alignment, news categories, and outlets.

\section{Data and Method}

\subsection{Dataset}

We drew 30,000 caption--article pairs from the VisualNews dataset
\citep{liu2021visualnews}, the largest publicly available corpus for news
image captioning research. The full dataset comprises 1,259,732 news items
from four English-language organisations (The Guardian, BBC, Washington Post,
USA Today), each including an image, its human-written caption, full article
text, and metadata (topic category, source identifier). Our analysis focuses
on Guardian and BBC, the two sources with sufficient coverage across all six
categories, and uses a stratified sample of 5,000 pairs per category:
politics and government, sport, business and economy, science and technology,
entertainment and culture, and world and society. The resulting corpus
contains 23,013 Guardian and 6,987 BBC pairs, accessed via the Hugging Face
Datasets library.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

All text underwent Unicode NFKC normalisation, HTML/XML tag removal, HTML
entity decoding, and whitespace standardisation. Lemmatisation and stopword
removal were applied selectively: Jaccard similarity operated on lemmatised
token sets, while SBERT processed raw text to preserve contextual meaning.
Captions were retained in full. Article texts were truncated to the first
512 whitespace-separated tokens for TF-IDF and Jaccard, and to 128 tokens
for SBERT (the model's optimal encoding range), following the inverted
pyramid convention in which the most caption-relevant content appears in
opening paragraphs \citep{yang2021journalistic}. Transformer-based spaCy
models (\texttt{en\_core\_web\_trf}) processed captions; the lighter
\texttt{en\_core\_web\_sm} model was used for articles where only POS
lemmas or NER were needed, reducing peak memory.

\subsection{Similarity Metrics}

We computed three complementary similarity measures for each
caption--article pair, following \citet{Gomaa2013}'s recommendation to
integrate lexical and semantic approaches.

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{2pt}

\item \textbf{TF-IDF cosine} ($s_{tf}$): sklearn
  \texttt{TfidfVectorizer} with unigrams and bigrams
  ($\mathrm{min\_df} = 2$, $\mathrm{max\_df} = 0.95$) fit on the combined
  caption + article corpus; per-pair cosine similarity. The
  multi-word n-gram range addresses named entities prevalent in news
  captions \citep{liu2021visualnews}; inverse-frequency weighting follows
  \citet{SparckJones1972TermSpecificity}.

\item \textbf{Jaccard} ($s_{jac}$): $|C \cap A|\;/\;|C \cup A|$ on
  lemma sets derived from the same spaCy pass; 0 if the union is empty.
  This provides a set-theoretic vocabulary overlap measure without
  frequency weighting.

\item \textbf{SBERT} ($s_{sbert}$): cosine similarity of
  \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} sentence embeddings \citep{Reimers2019},
  capturing semantic relatedness beyond surface-form overlap, consistent
  with \citet{Qu2024VACNIC}'s finding that effective news captions draw
  heavily on article-level context.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Structural Feature Extraction}

Structural features were extracted from captions using the spaCy
transformer pipeline, organised into three groups.

\textbf{Length:} \textit{token count}, \textit{word count},
\textit{character count}, \textit{sentence count}.

\textbf{Syntactic complexity:} \textit{average sentence length},
\textit{dependency parse depth} (maximum tree depth in the dependency
parse), \textit{clause count} (number of clausal dependency relations
plus one).

\textbf{Vocabulary:} \textit{type--token ratio} (TTR, on alphabetic
tokens), \textit{content word proportion} (NOUN/VERB/ADJ/ADV ratio),
\textit{proper noun proportion}.

\textbf{POS overlap:} The proportion of caption lemmas of each POS tag
(NOUN, VERB, ADJ, PROPN) whose surface or lemmatised forms also appear in
the article: $|C_{pos} \cap A_{pos}|\;/\;|C_{pos}|$, set to 0 when the
caption set is empty.

Multicollinearity among length features (token count, word count, character
count) was expected by design; to mitigate this in the multivariate
regression, only \textit{word count} was retained as the primary length
predictor, with \textit{average word length} (derived from character count
divided by word count) as a secondary length-quality measure. Variance
inflation factors (VIF) were computed for all predictors; no predictor
exceeded VIF~=~5.0.

\subsection{Caption Function Classification}

Captions were classified into four functional types based on the
taxonomy of \citet{marsh2003taxonomy} and the coherence framework of
\citet{Alikhani2023ImageTextCoherence}, using rule-based linguistic
indicators. Rules fire in priority order to assign a unique label per
caption; all criteria are independent of the similarity scores to avoid
circularity.

\textbf{Extractive:} A caption is classified as Extractive if it contains
quotation marks (direct speech markers), or if more than three of the
first five content-word lemmas of the caption appear among the article
lead lemmas --- provided no deictic markers are present. These captions
restate or directly echo article content.

\textbf{Descriptive:} A caption is classified as Descriptive if it
contains visual-location or scene-setting markers (e.g., ``pictured'',
``from left'', ``file photo'', ``above''), or if it opens with a person
name immediately followed by a visual-posture verb (e.g., ``poses'',
``stands'', ``sits'', ``appears'') --- provided no quotation marks are
present. These captions describe the image scene with limited article
connection beyond named entities.

\textbf{Expansive:} A caption is classified as Expansive if it contains
temporal context markers (``yesterday'', ``last week'', ``in [year]''\ldots),
causal or contrast connectives (``amid'', ``despite'', ``following'',
``after''\ldots), or relative clause markers (``\textit{,} who~'',
``\textit{,} which~'') --- provided no quotation marks or deictic markers
are present. These captions contextualise or elaborate beyond the article.

\textbf{Independent:} Default category; assigned when none of the above
rules fire. These captions carry content not directly derivable from the
article text.

To validate that these rule-based categories reflect natural data
structure rather than arbitrary linguistic distinctions, $k$-means
clustering ($k = 4$, matching the number of functional types) was applied
to the similarity and structural feature space. The four rule-based types
correspond to naturally separable clusters in this space (Figure~\ref{fig:cluster_val}),
lending convergent validity to the typology.

\subsection{Named Entity Alignment}

Named entities of four types --- PERSON, GPE (geo-political entities),
ORG, DATE --- were extracted from both captions and articles using the
spaCy transformer-based NER pipeline (\texttt{en\_core\_web\_trf}).
Entity types were chosen following \citet{liu2021visualnews}, who
demonstrate that these four types dominate news caption vocabulary.

To account for surface-form variation (e.g., ``Boris Johnson'' vs.\
``Johnson'', ``U.S.'' vs.\ ``United States''), fuzzy string matching
was applied using Levenshtein similarity with a threshold of $\geq 0.85$
(\texttt{rapidfuzz}). This threshold was chosen to permit minor surface
variations (hyphenation, middle names, abbreviations) while excluding
spurious matches between short dissimilar strings; a threshold of 0.80
introduced too many false positives for two-token entity pairs, while
0.90 missed common title-plus-surname variations.

For each caption--article pair, we computed: \textit{per-type overlap}
($|E_{cap} \cap E_{art}| / |E_{cap}|$ for each entity type, set to 0
when the caption set is empty), \textit{entity Jaccard} (symmetric set
overlap across all entity types combined), and \textit{entity coverage}
(overall proportion of article entities present in the caption). Pairs
with no caption entity of a given type contribute a zero to that type's
overlap column, so unconditional and conditional (entity-present) means
are both reported.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

Pearson $r$ was used for pairwise correlations (RQ1). Kruskal--Wallis
$H$ tests with $\eta^2 = (H - k + 1)/(n - k)$ were used for all group
comparisons (RQ2, RQ4, RQ5). Dunn post-hoc tests with Bonferroni
correction were applied for pairwise comparisons. Standardised OLS
regression (all predictors $z$-scored) was used to estimate unique
contributions of structural features, entity overlap, caption type, and
source/category controls to SBERT similarity (RQ1 synthesis). Significance
threshold: $\alpha = 0.05$ throughout.

\subsection{RQ1: Structural Features and Similarity}

Figure~\ref{fig:struct_heat} shows Pearson $r$ for each structural feature
against the three similarity metrics; non-significant cells ($p > 0.05$)
are marked \textit{ns}. Figure~\ref{fig:reg_coefs} shows standardised OLS
coefficients ($\hat{\beta}$) for SBERT as the outcome. Together, all
predictors explain 28.8\% of variance in SBERT ($R^2 = 0.288$),
confirming H1.

\textbf{POS overlap features} are the strongest and most consistent
predictors. Proper-noun overlap has the largest standardised coefficient
($\hat{\beta} = 0.170$, $t = 28.87$, $p < 0.001$), followed by noun
overlap ($\hat{\beta} = 0.101$, $t = 17.09$, $p < 0.001$). Both
correlate significantly with all three metrics ($r_{sbert} = 0.330$ and
$0.291$ respectively), indicating that captions sharing named referents
with their article are more aligned across all dimensions of similarity.
Proper-noun overlap also shows the highest TF-IDF correlation
($r_{tf} = 0.465$), consistent with named entities being high-weight
TF-IDF terms.

\textbf{Syntactic complexity} also predicts SBERT: dependency depth
($\hat{\beta} = 0.072$, $t = 10.21$, $p < 0.001$; $r_{sbert} = 0.140$)
and average sentence length ($\hat{\beta} = 0.048$, $t = 4.97$,
$p < 0.001$; $r_{sbert} = 0.121$) both reach significance, suggesting
that structurally richer captions tend to mirror the article more closely
at the semantic level.

\textbf{Caption length features} (word count, character count) correlate
strongly with Jaccard ($r \approx 0.57$) but weakly with SBERT
($r \approx 0.08$), a well-known length bias in set-overlap metrics.
Jaccard values should therefore be interpreted with caption length in mind.
In the multivariate model, word count carries a negative coefficient
($\hat{\beta} = -0.060$, $t = -5.54$, $p < 0.001$) after controlling
for overlap features, reflecting that longer captions with low overlap
contribute independent content.

\textbf{Proper-noun proportion} is negatively associated with SBERT in the
multivariate model ($\hat{\beta} = -0.102$, $t = -10.62$, $p < 0.001$),
even though proper-noun \textit{overlap} is the strongest positive
predictor. This reversal reflects a composition effect: captions dense
with proper nouns that do not appear in the article reduce alignment.

\textbf{Type--token ratio} (TTR) shows a negative univariate correlation
with Jaccard ($r_{jac} = -0.343$) but a positive coefficient in the
multivariate regression ($\hat{\beta} = 0.046$, $t = 6.92$, $p < 0.001$)
once length and overlap features are controlled. This sign reversal is
consistent with a Simpson's paradox: longer captions tend to have lower TTR
and higher Jaccard, so the raw negative correlation is length-driven rather
than reflecting a direct effect of lexical diversity on alignment.

\subsection{RQ2: Caption Function and Similarity}

Figure~\ref{fig:type_dist} shows the distribution of caption types.
The majority of captions are classified as \textbf{Independent}
(46.0\%, $n = 13{,}792$) --- carrying content not directly traceable to
the article text --- followed by \textbf{Extractive} (37.3\%,
$n = 11{,}198$), \textbf{Expansive} (14.1\%, $n = 4{,}231$), and
\textbf{Descriptive} (2.6\%, $n = 779$).

Caption type is strongly associated with similarity across all three
metrics (Kruskal--Wallis: SBERT $H = 5{,}823$, $\eta^2 = 0.194$;
TF-IDF $H = 5{,}417$, $\eta^2 = 0.181$; Jaccard $H = 7{,}659$,
$\eta^2 = 0.255$; all $p < 0.001$). Every pairwise Dunn comparison is
significant after Bonferroni correction ($p < 0.001$), supporting H2.

The ordering is consistent across all three metrics.
\textbf{Extractive} captions show the highest alignment by a wide margin
(SBERT mean~=~0.574, median~=~0.593): direct quotations and high lexical
overlap with the article are both diagnostic of Extractive classification
and independently drive high similarity scores.
\textbf{Descriptive} captions (mean~=~0.458, median~=~0.469) rank second;
visual-location language and named persons co-occurring with the article
provide moderate semantic overlap despite topical distance.
\textbf{Expansive} captions (mean~=~0.423, median~=~0.434) rank third;
temporal and causal markers introduce context beyond the article, reducing
direct lexical correspondence.
\textbf{Independent} captions show the lowest alignment (mean~=~0.405,
median~=~0.410), consistent with their defining characteristic of
introducing framing absent from the article body.

The large effect size for caption type ($\eta^2 = 0.194$ for SBERT;
$\eta^2 = 0.255$ for Jaccard) makes it the strongest single predictor of
similarity in this study, also reflected in the regression coefficient for
Extractive type ($\hat{\beta} = 0.308$, $t = 52.13$, $p < 0.001$;
Table~\ref{tab:reg}). The Expansive coefficient is small and non-significant
($\hat{\beta} = -0.008$, $p = 0.14$) in the multivariate model, indicating
that Expansive captions' lower similarity is largely accounted for by their
structural and overlap characteristics rather than function per se.

\subsection{RQ3: Named-Entity Alignment}

Figure~\ref{fig:entity_bar} shows per-type entity overlap, and
Figure~\ref{fig:entity_scatter} plots entity Jaccard against SBERT
similarity.

At the corpus level, unconditional means are suppressed by the high
proportion of captions containing no named entity of a given type:
50.3\% contain no PERSON, 66.1\% no GPE, 67.6\% no ORG, and 76.9\%
no DATE. Among captions that do name at least one entity, overlap rates
are substantially higher: PERSON~=~0.751, ORG~=~0.721, GPE~=~0.715,
DATE~=~0.535 (conditional means, based on $n = 15{,}087$, $n = 9{,}707$,
$n = 10{,}174$, and $n = 6{,}936$ pairs respectively). PERSON entities
have the highest raw mean overlap across all 30,000 pairs
(unconditional mean~=~0.378), confirming H3.

DATE overlap is substantially lower (conditional mean~=~0.535; raw
mean~=~0.124), consistent with captions frequently anchoring events to
a specific moment (``yesterday'', ``last week'') that does not match
the more varied or vague temporal expressions in the article body.
This pattern aligns with \citet{Tranetal2020}'s finding that ORG
entities are harder to preserve accurately in generation; our results
add that DATE overlap is also the weakest type in human-written captions,
suggesting a systematic journalistic tendency to use caption-specific
temporal framing.

Aggregate entity alignment (entity Jaccard, mean~=~0.042,
median~=~0.033) correlates moderately with SBERT ($r = 0.293$) and is
the third-largest regression predictor ($\hat{\beta} = 0.133$,
$t = 22.96$, $p < 0.001$; Table~\ref{tab:reg}), demonstrating that
entity overlap provides a meaningful alignment signal independent of
general vocabulary overlap. Entity alignment also follows the
caption-type ordering: Extractive captions show the highest entity
Jaccard (mean~=~0.055) and Independent the lowest (0.030), indicating
that the functional alignment captured by caption type is partly mediated
by named-entity sharing. H3 is supported.

\subsection{RQ4: News Category}

Figures~\ref{fig:cat_bar} and \ref{fig:cat_box}, and
Table~\ref{tab:kw}, show how similarity varies across the six topic
categories.

Differences are statistically significant for all three metrics
($p < 0.001$ in every case), though effect sizes are modest
($\eta^2 = 0.004$ for TF-IDF; $\eta^2 = 0.022$ for Jaccard;
$\eta^2 = 0.028$ for SBERT). \textbf{Sport} (median SBERT~=~0.524) and
\textbf{business and economy} (median~=~0.521) show the strongest
semantic alignment; the two are indistinguishable from each other
(Dunn $p = 1.000$). \textbf{Politics and government} (median~=~0.504)
and \textbf{science and technology} (median~=~0.478) are intermediate.
\textbf{World and society} (median~=~0.462) and \textbf{entertainment
and culture} (median~=~0.422) show the weakest alignment.

Bonferroni-corrected Dunn post-hoc tests confirm that every pair of
categories differs significantly ($p < 0.001$) except sport
vs.\ business ($p = 1.000$) and business vs.\ politics ($p = 0.010$).
Entertainment and culture differs from all other categories at
$p < 0.001$.

These results align with the hard/soft news framework
\citep{LehmanWilzig2010}: event-driven, factually dense categories
(sport, business) show the strongest caption--article coherence, while
entertainment --- closer to soft news in audience orientation --- shows
the weakest. The modest $\eta^2$ values indicate that journalistic format
conventions constrain similarity regardless of topic, limiting the
practical magnitude of category-driven differences. H4 is supported.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Caption Function as the Primary Driver}

The most striking finding is the dominance of caption function. With
$\eta^2 = 0.194$ for SBERT, caption type accounts for roughly seven
times more variance in semantic similarity than news category ($\eta^2 =
0.028$) and nearly a hundred times more than source ($\eta^2 = 0.002$).
This is consistent with \citet{Alikhani2023ImageTextCoherence}'s argument
that image--text pairs instantiate distinct pragmatic relations, but
extends that claim from image-caption pairs to caption--article triads:
the rhetorical function a journalist assigns to a caption is the single
strongest determinant of its textual relationship to the article.

The high proportion of Independent captions (46.0\%) is noteworthy in
its own right. Nearly half of all news captions in this corpus introduce
content not directly derivable from the article, which has implications
for misinformation detection and multimodal summarisation: systems that
assume caption content is entailed by or paraphrasable from the article
will fail for almost half the pairs in this dataset. The 37.3\% Extractive
rate is similarly high; these captions directly borrow from article text
or quote sources, meaning lexical overlap metrics substantially
overestimate semantic novelty for over a third of the corpus.

The $\hat{\beta} = 0.308$ coefficient for Extractive type (Table~\ref{tab:reg})
dwarfs every structural predictor in the model, suggesting that the
Extractive--non-Extractive distinction is more important than any
continuous structural feature for predicting similarity. The rule-based
classifier captures this distinction with sufficient validity, as confirmed
by the k-means cluster alignment (Figure~\ref{fig:cluster_val}).

\subsection{Named Referents Drive Structural Alignment}

Among structural features, the prominence of proper-noun overlap
($\hat{\beta} = 0.170$) over all other features confirms the theoretical
intuition underlying entity-aware captioning research
\citep{biten2019goodnews, Tranetal2020, Qu2024VACNIC}: what connects a
caption to its article, at the structural level, is primarily the sharing
of named referents rather than topical vocabulary or syntactic richness.

The simultaneous positive effect of proper-noun overlap and negative
effect of proper-noun proportion ($\hat{\beta} = -0.102$) illustrates
the distinction between \textit{shared} and \textit{unshared} named
content. Captions that introduce many proper nouns not found in the
article --- as is typical of file-photo captions naming individuals only
tangentially related to the story --- reduce alignment even as they are
nominally entity-dense.

The TTR sign reversal and the weak negative coefficient for
content-word proportion ($\hat{\beta} = -0.019$, $p = 0.036$) together
suggest that higher lexical diversity and more dense content-word usage
in a caption, after controlling for overlap, are associated with greater
editorial distance from the article. Captions that diverge most
structurally from the article's vocabulary are not simply shorter or
simpler, but use a richer, more varied vocabulary --- consistent with
editorial decisions to frame or contextualise rather than restate.

\subsection{Entity Overlap as an Independent Signal}

Entity Jaccard ($\hat{\beta} = 0.133$; $r_{sbert} = 0.293$) adds
explanatory power beyond general vocabulary overlap, confirming H3 and
supporting the claim that named entity alignment is a semi-independent
dimension of caption--article coherence \citep{mullerbanek2021multimodal}.
PERSON entities have the highest unconditional overlap (mean~=~0.378)
and the highest conditional overlap when present (0.751), consistent
with \citet{Tranetal2020}'s finding that PERSON accuracy is the central
factor in caption coherence for generation tasks.

DATE entities show the lowest conditional overlap (0.535). This reflects
the common journalistic practice of using caption-specific temporal
framing (``yesterday'', ``at Wednesday's briefing'') that does not
correspond to the article's more varied temporal expressions, even when
both texts reference the same event. This temporal mismatch is a
structural feature of news captioning rather than a noise artifact, and
any automatic alignment system must handle it explicitly.

\subsection{Genre and Source Conventions}

The category effects (H4) are statistically robust but practically
modest, with sport and business showing indistinguishable coherence
(Dunn $p = 1.000$). The result that entertainment and culture is the
weakest category on all three metrics aligns with \citet{LehmanWilzig2010}
and \citet{Cheema2023}: soft-news categories permit greater editorial
latitude in caption framing, allowing captions to emphasise human
interest or evaluative angles that diverge from the article's primary
factual content. The uniformly low $\eta^2$ values across categories
suggest that journalistic training and house style impose a floor on
caption--article divergence regardless of topic, limiting the
practical relevance of category effects relative to caption function.

For source variation (H5), the near-zero SBERT $\eta^2 = 0.002$ --- with
a statistically significant but trivially small $H = 66.0$ --- indicates
that Guardian and BBC follow essentially the same editorial norms for
semantic caption--article coherence. The larger surface-metric differences
(TF-IDF $\eta^2 = 0.018$) are a length artefact: Guardian captions are
on average 5.5 words longer, mechanically inflating Jaccard and TF-IDF
scores. We cannot test the full UK-vs-US hypothesis (H5) without Washington
Post and USA Today data; however, the absence of meaningful UK-internal
source differences suggests that outlet-level culture may matter less
than genre conventions and caption function for determining alignment.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations bound the interpretation of these findings. First,
the corpus is restricted to Guardian and BBC, both UK broadsheet outlets;
generalisation to tabloid, broadcast, or US sources requires extension
to the full four-source VisualNews dataset. Second, the caption function
classifier is rule-based and was not validated against a human-labelled
gold standard beyond the k-means cluster convergence check; classification
errors in edge cases (e.g., captions that mix quoting and visual
description) may shift the RQ2 distributions. Third, article texts are
truncated to 512 tokens; for long investigative pieces, relevant content
for the caption may appear later in the article and be excluded.
Fourth, the 30,000-pair sample represents 2.4\% of the full VisualNews
corpus; while the stratified design ensures category balance, rare
caption types or unusual editorial practices may be underrepresented.
Finally, the study addresses only textual similarity; the image modality
is not incorporated, leaving open the question of whether
visually-grounded captions (those strongly prompted by image content
rather than article content) show different patterns.

\section{Conclusion}

We analysed caption--article textual similarity across 30,000 VisualNews
pairs using three complementary metrics, structural features, entity
alignment, and group comparisons by category and source. All five
hypotheses are supported.

Caption function (H2) is the strongest predictor of alignment
($\eta^2 = 0.194$ for SBERT): extractive captions achieve nearly 0.17
SBERT points above independent captions, confirming that the rhetorical
role assigned to a caption directly and substantially constrains its
textual relationship to the article. Nearly half the corpus (46.0\%)
consists of independent captions whose content is not directly traceable
to the article --- a finding with direct implications for misinformation
detection and multimodal summarisation systems that assume caption--article
entailment.

Among structural features (H1), proper-noun overlap dominates
($\hat{\beta} = 0.170$), with entity Jaccard providing a complementary
independent signal ($\hat{\beta} = 0.133$; H3). PERSON entities show
the highest alignment when present (conditional overlap 0.751), while
DATE entities show systematic under-alignment (0.535) attributable to
caption-specific temporal framing. News category effects (H4) are
statistically significant but modest ($\eta^2 = 0.028$): sport and
business show the highest coherence and entertainment the lowest,
consistent with the hard/soft news distinction. Source-level differences
(H5) are largely a caption-length artefact, with negligible semantic
alignment differences between outlets once length is controlled.

These findings have practical implications for automatic caption
generation, multimodal fact-checking, and news retrieval systems: caption
function classification and named-entity overlap capture more variance in
caption--article alignment than any single structural feature, and should
be treated as first-class signals in downstream models. Future work should
extend the analysis to Washington Post and USA Today to fully test H5,
evaluate the rule-based classifier against a human-labelled gold standard,
and incorporate image-modality signals to distinguish visually-prompted
from article-prompted caption content.
