\begin{abstract}

Image captions in news articles perform a dual role: they anchor a photograph
to the surrounding text while simultaneously providing a concise, self-contained
narrative. How closely a caption tracks the lexical and semantic content of its
parent article, and what factors predict that alignment, remain open questions.
We present a systematic, multi-dimensional analysis of caption--article textual
similarity using 2,520 aligned pairs drawn from the VisualNews dataset, spanning
four major English-language news outlets and five topical categories.
We extract structural linguistic features from captions, compute three

semantic similarity), perform named-entity alignment with fuzzy string matching,

\section{Introduction}

The relationship between a news photograph's caption and its accompanying
article text is fundamentally asymmetric: an article may run to hundreds of
words, while a caption rarely exceeds thirty \citep{alikhani-stone-2019-cite}.
Yet the two are expected to describe the same event.
Understanding \emph{how much} of the article's content a caption captures, and
through which linguistic mechanisms, has practical implications for automatic
caption generation, news summarisation, and multimodal fact-checking.

Most existing research has focused on caption generation like training models to produce captions from images and article text rather than analysing the textual relationship between human-written captions and their source articles. This leaves a significant gap of knowing relatively little about how captions relate linguistically to the articles they accompany, or how this relationship varies across news topics and publishers. This gap matters for several reasons like from a journalism perspective, understanding caption--article similarity can reveal editorial conventions and inform best practices in news writing and from a computational standpoint, characterising this relationship can improve downstream tasks such as misinformation detection, multimodal retrieval and automated captioning. This requires to systematically measure caption--article textual similarity across multiple dimensions like lexical, semantic, structural, and entity-based.

This paper addresses five research questions:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5em, style=nextline]
  \item[RQ1 (Structural Features)] Do structural linguistic features of captions (length, syntactic
    complexity, vocabulary richness) predict caption--article similarity?
  \item[RQ2 (Functional Classification)] Does the \emph{function} of a caption (e.g.\ descriptive,
    contextualising, evaluative) systematically vary in its degree of
    textual alignment with the article?
  \item[RQ3 (Named Entity Alignment)] Do named entities provide a reliable alignment signal, and
    does alignment differ by entity type?
  \item[RQ4 (News Category Effects)] Does caption--article similarity vary across news topics
    (politics, sport, business, science, entertainment)?
  \item[RQ5 (Cross-Source Variation)] Are there cross-publication differences in caption--article
    alignment, and do UK outlets differ from US outlets?
\end{description}

We test five corresponding hypotheses (H1--H5, detailed in
Section~\ref{sec:method}) using both distributional statistics and
inferential tests on a 2,520-item stratified sample from VisualNews.


\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Caption--image alignment.}
Early work on visual--linguistic grounding focused on image--sentence matching
\citep{Fang2015,Young2014}. More recently, large-scale datasets such as
VisualNews \citep{liu2020visualnews} and NewsCLIPpings \citep{luo2021newsclippings}
have enabled studies of caption veracity and cross-modal alignment.

COSMOROE is a corpus based framework for classifying semantic interrelations between images, language and body movements which is validated on a corpus of TV travel programmes. \cite{pastra2008} is one of the earliest theoretically grounded attempts to systematically categorize cross modal relationships which makes it a useful foundation for caption typology for functional classification into distinct types like descriptive, expansive, independent or extractive. However, its domain is audiovisual broadcast media and it offers no textual similarity metrics or caption specific functional categories. It provides temporary theoretical evidence but requires substantial adaptation for a text to text news caption context.

\cite{alikhani2020cross} annotates 10{,}000 image caption pairs with discourse inspired coherence relations and uses them to train coherence aware captioning models, which demonstrates improved caption consistency when functional caption type is considered. It offers a grounded typology of caption content relationships that closely relates to the descriptive / expansive / extractive / independent classification that this study aims to develop. The limitation is that it operates on image caption pairs in general domain datasets, not on text to text caption article relationships in news. Its coherence categories may not translate cleanly to more journalistic caption functions without deliberate adaptation.

\cite{biten2019goodnews} proposes a news image captioning model that selectively draws on article text guided by visual cues and dynamically incorporates out of vocabulary named entities and introduces the GoodNews dataset. It is highly relevant to RQ2 by showing that news captions are contextually grounded in article content in ways generic captions are not. By demonstrating that persons, locations and organizations from articles are regularly incorporated into captions it also aligns with RQ3. The core limitation is that captioning is treated as a generation task, so human written caption to article textual similarity is never measured and variation by news category or sources is not examined.

\paragraph{Textual similarity in news.}
\cite{wang2020measurement} reviews text similarity methods across two dimensions which are text distance and text representation, tracing the evolution from edit distance and Jaccard similarity to BERT and graph neural networks. This serves as the methodological backbone for informing metric selection across all sub questions. Its key limitation is being entirely domain agnostic with no treatment of news specific or short text similarity challenges. It also predates more recent sentence level models like Sentence BERT, which may be better suited for caption length text comparisons.

Lexical overlap metrics (BLEU, ROUGE) have been widely used for caption
evaluation \citep{Vedantam2015}. Embedding-based metrics such as BERTScore
\citep{zhang2020bertscore} and SBERT \citep{reimers-gurevych-2019-sentence}
offer complementary, semantics-sensitive assessments.

Gomaa and Fahmy \cite{Gomaa2013} categorize text similarity algorithms into three types: string based, corpus based and knowledge based and emphasize the significance of hybrid approaches. This encompasses fundamental lexical matching techniques such as TF--IDF and cosine similarity, corpus based semantic models such as LSA, along with WordNet based semantic computation methods and tools. Reimers and Gurevych \cite{Reimers2019} present Sentence-BERT, which employs a siamese network architecture to generate efficient and semantically robust sentence embeddings, significantly improving performance on semantic textual similarity assessments while reducing computational costs. Together, these studies establish a developmental trajectory from superficial lexical matching to contextual semantic modeling, offering methodological foundations for our combination of TF--IDF, Jaccard and SBERT in similarity design and directly facilitating the assessment of similarity within structural features.

Sparck Jones' \cite{SparckJones1972TermSpecificity} seminal paper on term specificity provides the foundational rationale for using weighted lexical similarity as one component of our caption article similarity pipeline. She argues that the specificity of an index term should be defined statistically as a function of its distribution in a collection and shows that weighting terms inversely by collection frequency yields marked improvements in retrieval effectiveness compared with unweighted matching. This interpretation underlies TF--IDF style representations which we adopt as our primary bag of words similarity baseline. Our study extends beyond this work by combining such lexical weighting with structural features and sentence level semantic embeddings and by applying these measures to cross modal coherence rather than ad hoc document retrieval.

\paragraph{Named entity alignment.}

Entity-level overlap has been used as a factuality proxy in
summarisation \citep{Nan2021} and in cross-modal misinformation detection
\citep{Papadopoulos2023}. We extend this approach to caption--article pairs
with fuzzy matching to accommodate surface-form variation.

\cite{mullerbanek2021multimodal} quantifies coherence between news images and article text by applying named entity linking to extract persons, locations and events and then computing cross modal consistency scores, which is validated across datasets. These datasets cover multiple languages, topics and domains and demonstrate that consistency varies across topic domains including politics, sports, economy and travel. The key limitation is that the paper targets visual textual fake news detection, not linguistic analysis of caption article textual similarity, and the entity measures are grounded in computer vision rather than NLP based textual overlap.

\paragraph{Image--text relationship taxonomies.}
\cite{marsh2003taxonomy} constructs a taxonomy of 49 image to text relationship types across three broad categories derived from literature in journalism, education, children's literature and library science and validates it on nearly 1{,}000 image text pairs from news and web pages. It is foundational to RQ2 as one of the most comprehensive classification frameworks for how captions functionally relate to accompanying text, with journalism explicitly among its source domains. However, it addresses image text rather than text to text relations and provides no quantitative similarity metrics.

\cite{vempati2019understanding} proposes a framework of eight semantic image text relation classes which are characterized using cross modal mutual information, semantic correlation and status relation metrics with a deep learning model to predict the classes automatically. It advances the field by operationalizing functional caption text typologies into computationally controlled categories, thus moving beyond purely theoretical frameworks. The limitation is that the metrics rely on visual features that cannot be directly transferred to a text to text caption article framework and the classification is not evaluated within the news domain or across sources and categories.

\paragraph{Journalistic captioning conventions.}
\cite{yang2021journalistic} proposes a news captioning model that explicitly encodes journalistic writing conventions, using caption structure as a representational and generative constraint, which achieves state of the art performance on caption generation and named entity metrics. It informs RQ1 by showing that caption structure follows professional conventions and by demonstrating that journalistic captions are systematically context dependent through its entity focused evaluation. Its primary limitation is the generation framing, as it does not measure textual similarity between human written captions and articles nor does it analyse variation by news category.

\paragraph{News genre and editorial effects.}
\cite{LehmanWilzig2010} proposed a third category of news, integrated news, which is defined as important but not urgent content that falls between high impact hard news like politics and the economy and light, soft news like gossip and human interest stories. Integrated news addresses important but not urgent issues that are relevant to specific audiences. The category offers a useful framework for investigating category effects, by analysing the news in terms of urgency, factual density and audience targeting and indicates that news value can influence headline text coherence as well as the similarity patterns between headlines and body text across different news types.

\cite{Cheema2023} point out that meaning in news is shaped by news values rather than by simple redundancy between image and text. They combine semiotic analysis with journalism theory and emphasize that news values such as prominence, negativity and impact influence how different information is foregrounded. Moreover, this mechanism may vary between hard and soft news: hard news, driven by timeliness and factual impact, may be more inclined toward close alignment between textual and visual elements, whereas soft news, oriented toward human interest and emotional expression, may present more elaborative or interpretive relations. Based on this perspective, variation in similarity between captions and articles is systematically shaped by news categories and editorial orientations, thereby providing a theoretical foundation for our research questions on news category effects and cross source variation.

\paragraph{Entity-aware captioning models.}
The VisualNews paper introduces a large scale benchmark of more than one million news images paired with associated articles, human written captions and rich source metadata, and proposes an entity aware captioning architecture that explicitly targets accurate prediction of people, locations and events. Liu et al.\ \cite{liu2021visualnews} demonstrate that news images differ from generic captioning benchmarks because communicatively salient named entities dominate the caption vocabulary and they show that incorporating entity guided attention improves caption quality on both VisualNews and GoodNews datasets. For our purposes, VisualNews does two things. First, it empirically motivates RQ3 by framing entities as central to news caption informativeness, which justifies treating entity overlap and entity Jaccard as proxies for coherence between captions and articles. Second, its multi source composition and metadata highlight cross outlet variation but stop short of systematically quantifying how caption article similarity, entity alignment and structural properties vary across categories and news organizations, which is precisely what RQ4 and RQ5 are designed to address in our work.

Qu et al.\ \cite{Qu2024VACNIC} further sharpen the focus on entity and article alignment by showing that effective news captions typically encode more context from the article than from the image alone. Their framework combines a face naming module to improve name embeddings, CLIP based retrieval of article sentences that are semantically close to the image and a contrastive language model objective that aligns multimodal representations with a frozen language model space so that the caption generator prioritizes article relevant information. They report substantial gains over prior state of the art on GoodNews and NYTimes800k, which underscores that paying attention to article context and entity correctness is crucial for high quality news captions. However, their contribution remains model centric. They do not treat entity alignment or lexical similarity as objects of analysis in their own right, nor do they examine how human authored captions differ structurally or functionally across topics and sources. Our RQ3 therefore repurposes their insight about article centric context into an analytic agenda that measures named entity overlap and its relation to caption article similarity, rather than using it only as a training signal for generation.

Tran et al.\ \cite{Tranetal2020} propose an end to end entity aware news image captioning model, Transform and Tell, which integrates multimodal information from article text, images, faces and objects through a Transformer architecture to enhance the generation of named entities in news captions. The study constructs the NYTimes800k dataset and incorporates subword encoding and contextual language models, empirically demonstrating that the accuracy of named entity prediction particularly for PERSON entities is a central factor in caption coherence, while entity types such as ORG are more difficult to preserve accurately. This finding directly informs our research question on named entity alignment, particularly in examining the extent to which different entity types such as PERSON, GPE, ORG and DATE are preserved between captions and articles, and whether entity overlap can predict overall semantic similarity. Building on this insight, our study shifts from entity accuracy in caption generation to entity preservation and overlap in naturally occurring news discourse.

Finally \cite{alikhani2020cross} on image text coherence provide the main theoretical scaffold for how we conceptualize caption function and cross category variation. Drawing on discourse coherence theory, they argue that each image text pair instantiates a specific pragmatic relation, such as describing visible content, explaining circumstances of image creation, or expressing evaluation, and they show through case studies and modeling experiments that making these relations explicit supports better prediction, generation, and evaluation of multimodal content. Their framework is particularly important for our RQ2 and RQ4, because it motivates treating caption functions as systematic and classifiable rather than incidental, and it suggests that genre and topic can shift the distribution of coherence relations, for example between hard news and more narrative or evaluative coverage. At the same time, their analysis is primarily situated at the level of image caption pairs, not triadic image caption article constellations, and does not consider source level editorial cultures. Our study therefore adapts their coherence based perspective to a text analytic setting, operationalizing functional types (descriptive, expansive, independent, extractive) on captions and relating these to similarity scores, entity alignment, news categories, and outlets in order to trace how journalistic practice shapes caption article connections at scale.


\section{Data and Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Dataset}

We use VisualNews \citep{liu2021visualnews}, a large-scale dataset of
news images, captions, and full articles from four outlets: \emph{The Guardian},
\emph{BBC News}, \emph{USA Today}, and \emph{The Washington Post}.
After filtering to rows with non-empty article text, we retain 2,520 aligned

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

Text was Unicode-normalised (NFKC), stripped of HTML tags and entities, and
whitespace-standardised. Article text was truncated to the first 512
whitespace-delimited tokens to focus on the lead paragraph, which is most
likely to overlap with a caption. This threshold is predicated on the inverted pyramid structure of news writing, wherein the most critical information is presented in the initial paragraphs \citep{yang2021journalistic}.
Lemmatisation and stopword removal were applied selectively depending on the requirements of each downstream analysis module. For instance, Jaccard similarity operates on lemmatised token sets, while Sentence-BERT processes raw text in order to preserve contextual meaning.
Topic categories were consolidated from 30+ raw labels into five macro-categories:
\emph{politics\_government}, \emph{sport}, \emph{business\_economy},
\emph{science\_technology}, and \emph{entertainment\_culture}.

\subsection{Structural Feature Extraction}

All features are derived from captions using spaCy \texttt{en\_core\_web\_trf}.

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Length}: token count, word count (alphabetic tokens), character
    count, sentence count.
  \item \textbf{Syntactic complexity}: average sentence length, maximum
    dependency tree depth, clause count (clausal dependency relations $+ 1$).
  \item \textbf{Vocabulary}: type--token ratio (TTR) over alphabetic lowercased
    tokens; content-word proportion (NOUN, VERB, ADJ, ADV); proper noun
    proportion.
  \item \textbf{POS overlap}: for each of NOUN, VERB, ADJ, PROPN, the
    fraction of caption lemmas of that type that also appear in the article:
    $|L^{cap}_{pos} \cap L^{art}_{pos}| \;/\; |L^{cap}_{pos}|$, or 0 if the
    caption set is empty.
\end{itemize}

Pearson and Spearman correlations will be used to identify which features are most strongly associated with caption--article similarity.

\subsection{Similarity Metrics}

\paragraph{TF-IDF cosine ($s_{tf}$).}
A \texttt{TfidfVectorizer} (bigrams, $\text{min\_df}=2$, $\text{max\_df}=0.95$)
is fit on the combined caption+article corpus; per-pair cosine similarity is
computed between the caption and article vectors. This is founded on the principle of term specificity articulated by \cite{SparckJones1972TermSpecificity}, wherein infrequent terms are assigned greater weight than prevalent ones.

\paragraph{Jaccard similarity ($s_{jac}$).}
Both texts are lemmatised with spaCy; stopwords and punctuation are removed;
Jaccard similarity is computed over the resulting lemma sets:
$|C \cap A| \;/\; |C \cup A|$.

\paragraph{SBERT similarity ($s_{sbert}$).}
Caption and article embeddings are produced by
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} \citep{reimers-gurevych-2019-sentence} with
\texttt{batch\_size=64}; cosine similarity is computed over $\ell_2$-normalised
vectors. This is corroborated by \cite{Qu2024VACNIC}'s finding that effective news captions incorporate more contextual information from the article than from the image alone.

The interrelations among the three metrics will be analysed to determine convergent validity, focusing specifically on instances of high semantic similarity coupled with low lexical overlap.

\subsection{Caption Function Classification}

Captions are classified into four functional types: Extractive, Descriptive, Expansive and Independent. We use the taxonomy of image-text relationships defined in \citep{marsh2003taxonomy} and the coherence framework from \citep{Alikhani2023ImageTextCoherence} as theoretical grounding.

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Extractive} captions reiterate the content of the article.
  \item \textbf{Descriptive} captions limit themselves to describing the image and have low lexical overlap with their corresponding articles.
  \item \textbf{Expansive} captions add more context and background information not present in the article.
  \item \textbf{Independent} captions describe their images but have no meaningful connection to their article.
\end{itemize}

We classify captions using rule-based linguistic indicators including deictic markers, temporal connectives, quotation patterns, and named entity presence. We extract these features using spaCy. We assign a dominant category for each caption. All criteria are kept independent of similarity scores to avoid circularity. We use rule-based classification and unsupervised k-means clustering to validate whether the categories reflect natural patterns in the data.

\subsection{Named-Entity Alignment}

Entities of type PERSON, GPE, ORG, and DATE are extracted with
\texttt{en\_core\_web\_trf}. This focus is motivated by findings that named entities dominate news caption vocabulary \citep{liu2021visualnews} and are key to caption accuracy \citep{biten2019goodnews}. Two entity strings are considered a match if their
Levenshtein similarity $\geq 0.85$ (computed via \texttt{rapidfuzz}).
Per entity type we report \emph{overlap rate}
$= |E^{cap} \cap E^{art}| / |E^{cap}|$;
globally we report \emph{entity Jaccard}
$= |E^{cap} \cap E^{art}| / |E^{cap} \cup E^{art}|$
and \emph{coverage rate} $= |E^{cap} \cap E^{art}| / |E^{art}|$.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

Pearson $r$ (two-tailed) is used for correlation tests.
Group comparisons use the Kruskal--Wallis $H$ test followed by
Dunn's post-hoc test with Bonferroni correction for pairwise source
comparisons. Effect size is reported as
$\eta^2 = (H - k + 1) / (n - k)$.
Significance threshold: $\alpha = 0.05$.

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5em, style=nextline]
  \item[H1] At least one structural feature (esp.\ word count or TTR)
    significantly correlates with caption--article similarity.
  \item[H2] Caption function (type) significantly predicts similarity across
    all three metrics.
  \item[H3] Entity overlap rates differ by type; PERSON overlap is highest
    given its specificity.
  \item[H4] Caption--article similarity varies significantly across news
    categories, with sport showing higher alignment due to precise event
    descriptions.
  \item[H5] UK outlets (Guardian, BBC) exhibit higher caption--article
    similarity than US outlets (WaPo, USA Today).
\end{description}
